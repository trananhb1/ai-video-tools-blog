Step 1: Export your Sora data before April 26 (do this today)

Every video you generated on Sora is stored on OpenAI's servers and will be deleted on April 26, 2026. You have 5 days from today (April 21, 2026) to download your archive. OpenAI is processing exports in batches and turnaround is now 24–48 hours, so request yours immediately — if you wait until April 25 you may miss the cutoff.

  1. Sign in to Sora. Go to sora.chatgpt.com and log in with the same OpenAI account you used to generate videos. ChatGPT Plus, Pro, and Team accounts all have export access.
  2. Navigate to the Exports page. Open sora.chatgpt.com/exports/me directly. You can also reach it from the avatar menu > Settings > Data Controls > Export.
  3. Click "Request Export". OpenAI queues the job and emails the account on file when the archive is ready. Current turnaround as of April 21, 2026 is roughly 24–48 hours; expect this to slow as the deadline approaches.
  4. Download the ZIP. The email contains a single download link valid for 7 days. The archive includes every video you generated (MP4), the prompt that produced each one, generation timestamps, and any reference images you uploaded.
  5. Save the prompts separately. Copy your prompts into a plain text file or spreadsheet before you delete the ZIP. They will be useful when you test prompts on Kling, Runway, or Veo 3.1 — each platform handles prompts differently, but starting from your existing prompt library is faster than rewriting from scratch.
  6. API users: The Sora API stays live until September 24, 2026. Use the /v1/sora/generations endpoint to programmatically pull every generation tied to your API key. No new API keys are being issued, so existing integrations are the only way to retrieve assets at scale.

What about Sora subscriptions and refunds? OpenAI is automatically issuing prorated refunds for any unused portion of your Sora subscription. You don't need to request anything — the credit appears on your next billing cycle. Source: OpenAI Help Center.

Why is OpenAI shutting down Sora?

On March 24, 2026, OpenAI announced that Sora would shut down in two phases. The decision was driven by four factors:

  • Unsustainable compute costs. Video generation consumed GPU capacity that OpenAI needed for its core language model products.
  • Declining users. Active users dropped from 1 million at peak to under 500,000, according to TechCrunch reporting.
  • Copyright headwinds. Ongoing litigation over training data created legal risk that OpenAI chose not to carry.
  • Enterprise and IPO pivot. OpenAI is redirecting resources toward coding agents and enterprise products ahead of its planned IPO.

The practical impact: every creator, marketer, and developer who relied on Sora needs a replacement before April 26. Full migration playbook: prompts, testing, and stack combinations

You already have the export step (above) and the workflow matrix (further up). This section covers the parts of a Sora migration that catch people out: how to translate your prompts to a new platform, how to test fast without burning credits, and which two-tool combinations work better than any single replacement.

Adapt your prompts — they don't transfer 1:1

Pasting a Sora prompt into Kling, Runway, or Veo will not give you identical results. Each generator parses prompts differently, and what worked on Sora's diffusion architecture often needs structural changes elsewhere:

  • Kling 3.0 rewards shorter, structured prompts. Specify character details (clothing, age, ethnicity, features) explicitly. Western cultural references can be hit-or-miss.
  • Runway Gen-4.5 uses a style reference system — pair text prompts with reference images for consistent characters across multiple shots. The text-only prompt on Runway is weaker than Sora's was.
  • Veo 3.1 handles long, descriptive prompts well and is the strongest model for camera direction terms ("dolly shot", "rack focus", "low-angle tracking shot"). Closest to Sora's prompt-handling style.
  • Pika 2.5 works best with concise action-focused prompts. Avoid overloading with detail or it produces flatter output.
  • HeyGen and Synthesia use scripts, not prompts — you write what you want the avatar to say, not what you want the scene to look like. The mental model is fundamentally different from Sora.

Test smart: 5 prompts, 3 platforms, 0 dollars

Every tool in this guide has a free tier or trial that doesn't require a credit card. Don't commit to a paid plan until you've run your five most representative Sora prompts on two or three platforms. This 30-minute exercise will tell you more than any review can. Suggested triage:

  • Pure cinematic text-to-video: Test Kling (66 free credits) and Runway (125 free credits) head-to-head on the same prompt.
  • Avatar / talking-head: Test HeyGen (3 free videos/mo) with the same script you used on Sora.
  • Voiceover-driven content: Test Fliki (5 free min/mo) by pasting an old Sora prompt as a script.
  • Short-form / social: Test Pika (80 free credits) for fast iteration, then add Submagic for captions.

Two-tool stacks beat any single Sora replacement

Sora was a generalist. Most former users get better results combining two specialists than searching for a single drop-in replacement. Battle-tested combinations as of April 2026:

  • Kling + Pika (~$14/mo combined): Kling for hero content (3-min clips, 4K) + Pika for rapid social clips. Covers 80% of former Sora workflows at the lowest price.
  • HeyGen + Submagic (~$36/mo annual): Avatar V hero clips + animated captions for short-form. The standard solo-creator stack.
  • Runway + Pictory ($35/mo combined): Cinematic generation + auto-summarization for long-to-short repurposing.
  • Fliki + Arcads (~$131/mo): Multilingual voiceover content + premium AI UGC ads. Best for brand teams running paid social globally.
Pro tip: If you only have time to set up one tool before April 26, default to HeyGen's free tier. It covers the largest single Sora use case (talking-head spokespeople), takes 3 minutes to sign up, and you can upgrade to a paid plan after you've tested the workflow.
